## Why Seeds?

Humans can't eat gold.

That's the sentence I keep coming back to when people ask why I started SeedCoin instead of just buying Bitcoin. Bitcoin is a brilliant invention — I own some, I respect it. But its value is entirely a function of consensus. Take away the consensus, and you have nothing. That's fine for a speculative asset. It's less fine for something meant to underpin civilization.

Gold worked for centuries because it was rare, indestructible, and portable. Those are all real properties with real value. But they don't make gold *essential*. Humans could theoretically survive without gold. They cannot survive without seeds.

<PullQuote>
"Humans can't eat gold. They can't plant a Bitcoin. But they've been planting seeds for ten thousand years, and they'll be planting them long after every fiat currency has been forgotten."
</PullQuote>

## The oldest ledger

Before clay tablets, before cuneiform, before any written language, humans tracked value in seeds. They stored grain in ceramic jars as insurance against bad harvests. They traded seed varieties across the Silk Road with as much care as silk itself. The Svalbard Global Seed Vault — buried in a Norwegian mountain at -18°C — is the direct descendant of ten thousand years of seed banking.

Seeds are:

- **Scarce** — not infinitely replicable. Each variety's genetic information is unique, encoded over millennia of selection.
- **Useful** — they grow food. No other asset class does this.
- **Distributed** — stored in dozens of vaults across every continent.
- **Auditable** — you can literally count them.

These are exactly the properties you want in a reserve asset.

## The problem with abstractions

Modern currency has become increasingly abstract. We moved from gold to paper backed by gold, to paper backed by government promise, to digital numbers backed by... other digital numbers. Each step of abstraction created efficiency and scale. It also created fragility and, eventually, a currency that most people don't believe in.

This isn't nostalgia. I'm not arguing we should go back to barter or commodity exchange. Blockchain is a genuinely new and powerful technology — a way to maintain trustless ledgers without requiring centralized institutions. What I'm arguing is that the *anchor* for that ledger should be something real.

A cryptocurrency backed by consensus alone is more honest than fiat — at least the consensus is visible. But a cryptocurrency backed by seeds held in certified vaults, auditable by anyone with a flight to Norway, is better still.

## What SeedCoin is

SeedCoin is a Litecoin fork with one fundamental difference: the long-term vision is to back SEED tokens with real seeds held in real vaults. Not a metaphor. Not a PR strategy. Actual seeds, in actual cold storage, with actual custody documentation.

This is hard. The legal framework for real-world asset backing didn't exist in 2013. The DeFi infrastructure didn't exist. The partnerships with seed banks hadn't been negotiated. None of that was built yet.

We're building it now.

The 2013 version of SeedCoin was a proof of concept — a community and a thesis. The 2024–2026 version is the infrastructure to make that thesis a reality: smart contracts, custody agreements, on-chain proof of reserves, and a stablecoin.
</Callout>

## A word on skepticism

I expect — and welcome — skepticism. Crypto is full of projects that make grand claims and deliver nothing. Please apply that skepticism to SeedCoin.

Ask: do the seeds exist? (Not yet — we'll tell you when they do.)
Ask: is the smart contract audited? (Not yet — we'll tell you when it is.)
Ask: who controls the custody agreements? (The SeedDAO — here's how to verify.)

We will answer every hard question you have. The answers won't always be satisfying. Some of them will be "not yet." But we'll tell you the truth.

The seeds are real. The thesis is real. The timeline is honest.
